Section 01 · The Why

Why advising matters.

Before we get into what the Scope & Sequence is, we have to talk about why it exists. Kentucky has a workforce challenge — and structured advising is how we solve it. ✨

The real problem

Kentucky ranks 48th nationally in workforce participation. We have jobs going unfilled in growing industries like aerospace, advanced manufacturing, and healthcare — and we have students leaving high school without a clear plan for how to access them. The gap between those two facts is what advising closes.

The challenge isn't a lack of opportunities. The challenge is that too many Kentucky students never learn those opportunities exist, never see themselves in them, and never get a structured path to reach them. That's not a student problem. That's a systems problem — and systems problems require systemic solutions.

Why advising as moments doesn't work

Here's what advising looks like in too many Kentucky schools today:

  • A senior shows up at their counselor's office in October to talk about FAFSA. It's their first real conversation about financial aid in four years of high school.
  • An 8th grader picks high school courses based on what their friends are taking, not on a CTE pathway connected to their interests.
  • A 10th grader takes the ACT cold, with no preparation or context for why it matters.
  • A junior with strong grades doesn't apply for KEES money she's already earning, because no one ever explained how the program works.
  • A capable student decides college "isn't for them" — without ever exploring apprenticeships, technical credentials, or community college pathways that might actually be a perfect fit.

None of these students lacked talent or motivation. They lacked structured guidance, delivered consistently, over time. When advising happens in isolated moments — a single college fair, a rushed scheduling session, a senior-year scramble — students miss the cumulative effect of seeing how their education connects to their future. The result? Some students get great advising at home, from family members who know how the system works. Others don't. And the gap that creates is one of the largest predictors of postsecondary success in Kentucky.

💡
The big idea

Advising isn't an event. It's a system. When advising is structured — building progressively from middle school through 12th grade, woven into the school day, and reinforced by every adult in the building — outcomes change. Students who experience high-quality advising are significantly more likely to pursue and complete postsecondary education.

What structured advising changes

Imagine the same students in a school where the Scope & Sequence is the operating system:

  • The 6th grader takes a career interest survey, builds an initial ILP, and starts thinking about what work she might enjoy as an adult — long before high school course selection forces a real decision.
  • The 8th grader tours the local Area Technology Center, learns about EPSOs, and meets with a counselor to plan 9th-grade courses aligned to a CTE pathway he's actually interested in.
  • The 10th grader uses Futuriti's Net Price Calculator to see what college actually costs in Kentucky and starts a free postsecondary readiness exam plan.
  • The 11th grader builds a senior-year financial aid timeline in October — not December — and has time to apply for scholarships before deadlines.
  • The 12th grader files the FAFSA on time, applies to her postsecondary path with a network she's been building since 9th grade, and graduates with an actual plan rather than a vague hope.

Same students. Different outcomes. The difference isn't talent. It's structure.

Section 02 · Framework

A roadmap from 6th to 12th.

The Postsecondary Advising Scope & Sequence is a structured progression of advising experiences that builds knowledge and confidence year after year. It's not a curriculum — it's a flexible framework. 🗺️

What a scope & sequence actually is

If the term "scope & sequence" sounds like education jargon, that's because it is — but the concept is simple. Scope is what gets covered. Sequence is the order in which it's covered. Together, they answer the question: "What should every student learn, and when should they learn it?"

Most academic subjects have one. Math has a scope & sequence — you learn fractions before algebra, algebra before calculus. ELA has one. Even PE has one. But for years, postsecondary advising in Kentucky did not. Each school made it up. Each counselor improvised. The result was wildly inconsistent advising across the state — some students got incredible support, others got almost none.

The KAA Scope & Sequence changes that. It gives every Kentucky educator a shared answer to "what should advising look like in 6th grade? in 9th? in 11th?" — while leaving room for each school to deliver it in the way that works for their students, schedule, and culture.

Two guides, one framework

The Middle School (6–8) guide is organized by a monthly advising calendar. Each month has a theme — Self-Discovery in September, Network in November, Dollars & Cents in December, and so on. Within each month, learning experiences scale up by grade. A 6th grader does a basic financial literacy vocabulary lesson; a 7th grader plays the Game of Life; an 8th grader uses the Family Budgeting Calculator. Same theme, deeper engagement.

The High School (9–12) guide is organized by five lesson plans per grade per year: one to revisit the career interest & strength survey, plus one for each of the four focus areas. They can be delivered separately once per quarter, all in one day, or stretched over a week — schools choose what works. Each grade has a distinct phase: 9th is Investigation, 10th is Connection, 11th is Decision, and 12th is Action.

Each grade has a specific phase that builds on the last. Here's how students move from exploration to action:

Notice the progression of verbs. Explore a wide world of possibilities. Prepare by connecting interests to options. Apply what you've learned to the next chapter. By 12th grade, students aren't choosing a future — they're implementing one they've been building for seven years.

Why we built it this way

  • Consistency for every student. Same baseline of high-quality advising regardless of school, zip code, or which counselor a student happens to get
  • Aligned with Kentucky Graduation Requirements and the Kentucky Academic Standards for Career Studies
  • Supports meaningful ILP completion. Every learning experience generates evidence that goes in a student's Individual Learning Plan
  • Designed for a whole-school approach. Advising isn't just the counselor's job — it's everyone's job
  • Flexible delivery. A counselor can push lessons into classrooms. An advisory teacher can deliver them in 20-minute chunks. A whole-school PD day can knock out a quarter at once
📚
Quick context · Not a curriculum

The Scope & Sequence tells you what to advise on and when. It doesn't dictate how. Each focus area has linked lesson plans that are ready to use — but they're suggestions. The framework is the spine; the muscle is yours.

Section 03 · Focus Areas

The four focus areas.

Every grade addresses these same four focus areas — what changes is the depth, complexity, and urgency. By the end of 12th grade, students have built well-rounded knowledge across all four. 🎯

These four focus areas aren't arbitrary. They emerged from research, employer feedback, and what Kentucky students actually need to succeed after high school. Together, they cover the knowledge (what's possible), the logistics (how to access it), the finances (how to pay for it), and the skills (how to thrive once you're there). Drop any one of them, and the system breaks down.

💼

Career Exploration

Interest surveys, career clusters, occupations research, and connecting interests to career paths.

Why it matters: Students can't aspire to careers they don't know exist. Career Exploration is the entry point — it's how students discover what work feels meaningful, and it provides the personal "why" that powers the rest of the journey.
🎓

Postsecondary Opportunities

Degree types, postsecondary pathways, early college options (EPSOs), and application processes.

Why it matters: "Postsecondary" is bigger than four-year college. This focus area teaches students that apprenticeships, technical credentials, two-year degrees, military pathways, and certifications are all legitimate options.
💰

Financial Literacy

Budgeting, saving, credit, scholarships, FAFSA, and connecting education to financial outcomes.

Why it matters: Cost is the #1 reason Kentucky students don't pursue postsecondary education — even when they qualify for aid. Financial Literacy makes the invisible visible: KEES money, FAFSA, scholarships.
🤝

Essential Skills

Social capital, goal-setting, networking, leadership, and professional communication.

Why it matters: Knowing what you want isn't enough — students need durable skills that translate across every path. Building a network, communicating professionally, setting goals — these determine whether a plan actually happens.
📋

Notice the spiral

Look at all seven grades together and you'll see the same focus areas appearing again and again — but each time, deeper. A 6th grader takes a career interest survey to discover what jobs exist. A 12th grader retakes it as part of a final reflection on a journey that started six years earlier. A 6th grader maps three supportive adults. A 12th grader sends a networking email to a professional in their field. Same skill. Different stakes.

This is what curriculum designers call a "spiral curriculum" — concepts revisited at increasing levels of complexity. It's how learning sticks. It's also how a student who got a weak career exploration lesson in 6th grade still gets four more chances to build that knowledge before graduation.

Section 05 · Futuriti

Meet Futuriti.

Futuriti.org is the engine that powers the Scope & Sequence. It's Kentucky's first-of-its-kind career and college platform — and the tool your students will return to from middle school through graduation. 🧭

🌟 Launched February 2025

Where Kentuckians find their future.

Kentucky became the first state in the nation to turn its postsecondary and occupational data into a student-facing website. Built by CPE in partnership with KYSTATS and the Kentucky Department of Education.

Visit Futuriti.org →

Why Futuriti exists

Before Futuriti, Kentucky students researching careers had two options: generic national tools that didn't reflect Kentucky's economy, or scattered state databases that weren't built for them. A 9th grader curious about nursing could find national average salaries — but not what nurses actually earn in Pikeville versus Louisville.

Futuriti closes that gap. It's a Kentucky-specific platform that pulls together career data, college information, and pathway details into one place — and presents them in a way that students, families, and counselors can actually use. Nationally, it's a first: no other state has built a tool like this. And it's free to every Kentuckian.

How Futuriti shows up across grades

Futuriti isn't a one-time tool — it's a through-line. Students return year after year, with deeper questions each time.

  • Middle School: Career cluster exploration, vocabulary building, "what jobs exist in Kentucky?" research
  • 7th Grade — AI College Major Search: Students use Futuriti's career data alongside AI tools
  • 8th Grade — Career & Lifestyles: Family Budgeting Calculator and lifestyle research
  • 9th Grade — CTE Pathway Exploration: Students look up what pathways their HS and local ATC offer
  • 10th Grade — Net Price Calculator: A direct lesson built around this Futuriti tool
  • 11th Grade — My Next Move & College Research: Students compile college/career interest list using Futuriti data
  • 12th Grade — Postsecondary Applications: Final research before applying

Imagine a student who has used Futuriti every single year. By 12th grade, they're not learning a new tool — they're using a familiar one to make their biggest decision yet. That continuity is the point.

What this means for you

Futuriti only works as well as students' familiarity with it. A student who first sees Futuriti in March of senior year will use it differently than a student who's been on the site since 6th grade. Your job — regardless of role — is to make Futuriti visible and habitual. That can look like a one-time intro at a family night, a recurring assignment in 9th-grade English, a counselor pushing into classrooms with a 15-minute walkthrough, or a school-wide "Futuriti Day" once a quarter.

Section 06 · Student Game Plan

The Student Game Plan.

The Student Game Plan Template is the ILP companion that ties the entire Scope & Sequence together. Every grade-level lesson generates evidence that goes here. 📋

Why ILPs haven't always worked

Kentucky has required Individual Learning Plans for years. So why hasn't every Kentucky graduate left high school with a coherent plan? Because in many schools, the ILP became a compliance document — a thing students clicked through once a year, not a tool they actually used.

The Student Game Plan Template fixes this. It's not a separate ILP — it's a structured companion to whatever ILP system your school uses. It gives students a place to consolidate the evidence they generate across years of advising lessons, with tabs that mirror how a young person actually thinks about their future.

Five tabs, one plan

👋

Start Here

Personal intro: what I like, what I'm good at, career ideas

💡
02
My Career Interests

Survey results + postsecondary planning by grade

📝
03
My Activities

Game plan activities, evidence, and big ideas

📚
04
My Classes

Course planning + dual credit/AP/IB tracker

🤝
05
My Network

The people supporting my journey

Each tab has a specific job. Start Here grounds the student in self-knowledge. My Career Interests tracks their evolving thinking about work and postsecondary plans. My Activities is where they upload evidence from Scope & Sequence lessons. My Classes tracks course planning and credit accumulation, including EPSOs like dual credit, AP, and IB. My Network grows from a list of three supportive adults in 6th grade to a real professional network by graduation.

✨
Why this matters

Across every Scope & Sequence lesson, students are creating evidence that uploads to a tab in the Student Game Plan. This is what makes ILPs meaningful. Without the Game Plan, lessons disappear into a folder. With it, students see their own progression — survey results that change, networks that grow, courses that build toward something specific.

By 12th grade, a student's Game Plan is a document of who they're becoming — not a form they had to fill out.

What's needed to make it real

  • Time to use it. Students need dedicated time during the school day to update their Game Plan — 15 minutes once a month is enough.
  • Adults who reference it. Counselors, advisors, and teachers should refer to the Game Plan in conversations with students.
  • Family awareness. The Game Plan is most powerful when families know it exists and can ask their student about it.

None of that requires new technology, new curriculum, or new staff. It requires intention and consistency.

Section 07 · Whole-School Approach

A whole-school system.

Advising isn't a counselor's job alone — it's a shared responsibility across the school. The Scope & Sequence is delivered through four interconnected layers. 🏫

Why everyone has to play

Most American high schools have one counselor for every 400+ students. If postsecondary advising is delivered solely through one-on-one counselor meetings, the math doesn't work — there aren't enough hours in the year for a counselor to deliver five hours of structured advising to every student in every grade.

The Scope & Sequence solves this by treating advising as a school-wide system. Counselors design and lead, but they don't deliver it alone. Administrators set the conditions. Core and elective teachers weave career connections into their content. CTE instructors leverage work-based learning. Together, the four pillars cover what no single role could.

Schoolwide Culture

Administrators establish an advising culture and climate, setting a vision for what advising looks like throughout the school year by encouraging routines and setting expectations.

This is the most upstream of the four pillars. Without administrative commitment, the other three pillars struggle to function.

What it can look like

  • Coordinating school-wide events such as career fairs or family career nights
  • Setting aside time in the master schedule for advising activities
  • Communicating expectations for advising to be delivered by all school personnel
  • Celebrating ALL postsecondary plans equally — 4-year, 2-year, technical, military, apprenticeship, workforce

School Counseling

School counselors play a critical role designing school counseling programs that incorporate career and academic planning. They guide advising processes throughout the school.

Counselors are the quarterbacks — they know the playbook best and they coordinate the rest of the team.

What it can look like

  • Supporting educators in their efforts to advise students throughout the year
  • Pushing into classrooms to deliver academic and career planning content directly
  • Coordinating with parents and families to support students in academic and career planning
  • Serving as the school's lead on Scope & Sequence implementation

Core & Elective Courses

Educators in core and elective courses can play a role in delivering career exploration content by explicitly naming how certain topics or skills relate to real-world careers.

This is the most scalable pillar. Every English teacher mentioning that journalists, lawyers, marketing professionals, and screenwriters all use writing skills — that's career exploration happening 180 days a year.

What it can look like

  • Routinely (weekly or monthly) integrating career content within course curricula
  • Incorporating Essential Skills like goal-setting and communication into regular coursework
  • Making the "why does this matter for life beyond school?" question explicit in every unit
  • Using Futuriti for one assignment per semester

CTE Courses

CTE coursework naturally incorporates career content. It can also reinforce cross-curricular connections so students see the relationship between CTE and academic subjects.

CTE has been doing this work for decades — but its impact has often been confined to "CTE kids." The Scope & Sequence asks CTE programs to extend their reach.

What it can look like

  • Leveraging extracurricular opportunities like CTSOs (DECA, FBLA, SkillsUSA, FFA, HOSA)
  • Facilitating work-based learning that helps students explore careers and build social capital
  • Connecting CTE pathways to academic subjects so students see the full picture
  • Hosting "CTE for everyone" events that bring non-CTE students into the program for a day

The compounding effect

None of these four pillars can deliver the Scope & Sequence alone. But together, they create something powerful: a school where postsecondary advising isn't a destination, it's the air students breathe. Career connections in 1st period English. Quick wins for administrators

  • Protect the time. Build at least one regular advisory block into the master schedule.
  • Name an advising lead. A single point person to coordinate Scope & Sequence implementation.
  • Host a family launch event. Introduce families to Futuriti.org, the Game Plan Template, and the Scope & Sequence in the first quarter.
  • Honor all paths. Add a "Postsecondary Plans" celebration that recognizes 4-year, 2-year, technical, military, and apprenticeship plans equally.
  • Make KEES visible. Students start earning KEES in 9th grade based on GPA. Communicate that early and often.

Quick wins for counselors

  • Pick one grade band to start. Don't try to roll out 6–12 in year one. Pilot with one grade.
  • Push into 2–3 classrooms a month. Co-teach a Scope & Sequence lesson with a willing teacher.
  • Make Futuriti the through-line. Use it consistently from middle school through 12th.
  • Audit your ILP routine. Are students actually using the Game Plan Template? Make one specific change this year.
  • Don't skip the 8th-grade transition. Get students to ATC tours, EPSO awareness, and KEES introduction before 9th grade.

Quick wins for core/elective teachers

  • Add one career connection per unit. "Here's how a [career] uses what we're learning today" — 60 seconds, massive impact.
  • Invite one guest per semester. A parent, alum, or community member from a career related to your subject.
  • Embed an Essential Skill. Pick one — goal-setting, professional communication — and teach it explicitly.
  • Use Futuriti for one assignment. Career research, wage comparisons, pathway exploration.
  • Be an "ILP champion" in your room. Remind students to upload work to their Game Plan when it fits.

Quick wins for CTE instructors

  • Leverage your CTSO. DECA, FBLA, SkillsUSA, FFA, HOSA — these are built-in career exploration.
  • Connect to academic teachers. Help core teachers see how their content shows up in your pathway.
  • Push work-based learning hard. Internships, job shadows, and apprenticeships are 11th-grade Scope & Sequence experiences.
  • Make industry credentials visible. A credential of value can launch a career directly.
  • Talk about the WorkReady Scholarship. Students paying for CTE dual credit need to know about it.
